IRS Mileage Audit Guide: How to Survive a Vehicle Deduction Audit

May 2026  ·  9 min read

The IRS audits roughly 0.4% of individual returns each year — but returns with Schedule C vehicle deductions face a substantially higher examination rate. Mileage deductions are one of the most frequently disallowed items in small business audits, not because taxpayers are dishonest, but because their records don't meet the specific documentation standard outlined in IRS Publication 463.

If you deducted vehicle expenses last year and received a CP2000 notice or a formal audit letter, this guide walks you through exactly what the IRS expects to see, what triggers vehicle-related scrutiny, and how to protect your deductions going forward.

What Triggers an IRS Vehicle Deduction Audit?

The IRS uses a statistical model called the Discriminant Inventory Function (DIF) to score returns for audit potential. High Schedule C vehicle deductions relative to your reported income are a known trigger. Several other patterns increase risk:

  • 100% business use claimed — The IRS is skeptical of any taxpayer who claims their vehicle is used exclusively for business with zero personal miles. If you own one car, this claim is difficult to defend.
  • Round numbers — Claiming exactly 10,000 or 15,000 business miles suggests estimation rather than contemporaneous tracking. Real logs have irregular numbers like 9,847 or 11,203.
  • Deduction exceeds plausible use — If you claimed 30,000 business miles but your total odometer for the year was 22,000, the math doesn't work.
  • High ratio to income — A $1,200 freelance income with a $4,800 vehicle deduction draws scrutiny.
  • Prior year disallowance — If the IRS disallowed vehicle expenses in a previous audit, your current-year return may be flagged automatically.

Note: Receiving a CP2000 notice is not the same as a formal audit. A CP2000 is an automated mismatch notice (often triggered by 1099s). A full vehicle audit typically comes as a CP75, Letter 566, or Letter 2205. The documentation requirements are the same in either case.

The 4 Elements Required by Publication 463

IRS Publication 463 specifies exactly what a contemporaneous mileage record must contain for each business trip. Miss any one of these four elements and your deduction can be partially or fully disallowed.

1

Amount

The number of miles driven for business on that specific trip. A weekly total or monthly estimate does not satisfy this requirement.

2

Time

The date of the trip. "Multiple trips in March" is not sufficient. Each trip needs its own date entry.

3

Place

The destination — at minimum the city or general area, or the name of the customer or location visited.

4

Business Purpose

A brief description of the business reason for the trip. "Client meeting" or "site inspection at 123 Oak St." qualifies. Blank or "business" alone does not.

The word "contemporaneous" is critical. Publication 463 states records should be recorded "at or near the time of the expenditure or use." The IRS has rejected reconstructed logs created during an audit, even when the taxpayer had supporting evidence like calendar entries and receipts.

What "Contemporaneous" Actually Means in Practice

The IRS doesn't require a paper logbook. Any method that captures the four elements at or near the time of each trip qualifies:

  • A mobile app that timestamps each trip automatically (like MyMilesAI)
  • A paper mileage logbook kept in the glove compartment
  • A spreadsheet updated daily or weekly
  • Calendar entries with trip details noted at the time of the appointment

What doesn't qualify: reconstructing your mileage from bank statements, Google Maps history, or memory during an audit. The Tax Court has upheld IRS disallowances in cases where taxpayers attempted to recreate mileage logs retroactively, even with substantial corroborating evidence.

Tax Court precedent: In Gattuso v. Commissioner and similar cases, the Tax Court has consistently disallowed mileage deductions when logs were reconstructed after the fact, even when the taxpayer's business activity was genuine and well-documented in other ways.

What to Do If You Receive an Audit Notice

If you receive a letter requesting substantiation of your vehicle deductions, do not panic. Follow these steps:

  1. Identify the tax year(s) under review. The IRS can generally audit returns up to 3 years back (6 years if substantial underreporting is suspected). Know which year's records you need.
  2. Gather your mileage log. Locate whatever record you maintained — app export, paper log, spreadsheet, or calendar. Export it in a format you can submit: PDF, CSV, or printed summary.
  3. Compile corroborating evidence. Your mileage log is the primary record, but supporting documents strengthen your case: appointment calendars showing client meetings, client invoices, Google Maps screenshots of routes, gas receipts near claimed business dates.
  4. Calculate your total odometer reading. The IRS may ask for your odometer at the start and end of the year, plus any repair records that document mileage. Check your maintenance records.
  5. Consider professional representation. For audits involving more than $5,000 in disputed deductions, an enrolled agent (EA) or tax professional can represent you before the IRS and often negotiate a better outcome than self-representation.

Audit Outcome Scenarios: What Happens If Your Records Are Incomplete

Record Quality Likely IRS Response Typical Outcome
Complete contemporaneous log (all 4 elements) Accept as filed No change
Partial log + strong corroboration (calendar, invoices) Negotiate partial allowance Some deductions allowed
Reconstructed log created after notice Heavy skepticism; may reject Likely disallowed
No records at all Full disallowance proposed Full deduction lost + interest
Cohan rule argument (no records) Discretionary partial estimate Uncertain; often unfavorable

Note on the Cohan rule: Established in Cohan v. Commissioner (1930), this doctrine allows courts to estimate deductions when records are incomplete. However, Congress specifically overrode the Cohan rule for vehicle, travel, meals, and entertainment expenses in IRC Section 274(d), meaning estimates are not accepted for these categories. You need substantiation.

Odometer and Vehicle Records

Beyond the per-trip log, the IRS may request:

  • Beginning and ending odometer readings for the tax year. Your annual oil change receipts often have these.
  • Total miles driven (business + personal + commuting). The business percentage is calculated as business miles ÷ total miles.
  • Vehicle ownership or lease documentation — title, registration, or lease agreement.
  • Date the vehicle was placed in service for business use, which affects depreciation if you're using the actual expense method.

Commuting Miles Are Never Deductible

One of the most common audit adjustments is the disallowance of commuting miles. Travel between your home and a regular place of business — even if you consider your business "mobile" — is commuting, not business driving. This applies even if you take business calls in the car or carry work materials.

The exception: if you have a qualifying home office under IRC Section 280A, your home is treated as your principal place of business. In that case, travel from home to a client site or temporary work location can be deductible. The home office deduction must itself be properly claimed and defensible.

The Bottom Line

Vehicle deduction audits are winnable when you have contemporaneous records. They're expensive and stressful when you don't. The IRS isn't trying to catch you — they're verifying that your deduction is real and properly supported. Give them the documentation they've specified in Publication 463 and your deductions stand.
