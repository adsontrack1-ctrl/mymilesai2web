CRA Mileage Rates 2026: What Canadian Self-Employed Workers Need to Know

May 2026  ·  8 min read

The Canada Revenue Agency updates its automobile allowance rates annually. For 2026, the rates are $0.73 per kilometre for the first 5,000 km of business travel, and $0.67/km for every kilometre after that. These rates apply whether you're self-employed claiming motor vehicle expenses on your business return, or an employee claiming on a T777 with a signed T2200 from your employer.

This guide covers who can claim, how the rates apply, which CRA forms you need, and how to keep a logbook that satisfies a CRA audit.

Note: These rates apply in all provinces and territories except the Yukon, Northwest Territories, and Nunavut, which have higher rates ($0.77/km and $0.71/km respectively).

Who Can Claim Motor Vehicle Expenses?

The CRA distinguishes between two types of claimants:

Self-Employed Individuals

If you operate a business or practice a profession as a sole proprietor, you claim motor vehicle expenses on your business activity statement (CRA Statement of Business or Professional Activities). This is the most common scenario for freelancers, contractors, realtors, tradespeople, couriers, and anyone operating their own business without incorporation.

Self-employed individuals claim a business-use percentage of their actual vehicle costs (gas, insurance, maintenance, registration, depreciation), OR they can use the per-kilometre allowance rates as a simpler proxy. Either way, you need a logbook to establish your total kilometres and business-use percentage.

Employees (T777 + T2200)

Employees can claim vehicle expenses only if their employment contract requires them to use their own vehicle, they do not receive a non-taxable allowance from their employer, and their employer completes and signs Form T2200 (Declaration of Conditions of Employment). The expenses are claimed on Form T777 (Statement of Employment Expenses).

The T2200 requirement is strict. Without a signed T2200, employees cannot claim vehicle expenses on their personal return. If your employer reimburses you at the CRA prescribed rate ($0.73/km and $0.67/km), that reimbursement is not taxable income — but it also means you can't claim additional vehicle deductions on top of it.

How the 5,000 km Threshold Works

The tiered rate applies to each individual claimant — not per vehicle, not per employer. If you drive 8,200 business kilometres in 2026, your allowance is calculated as:

CRA Allowance Calculation — 8,200 Business Km

First 5,000 km × $0.73 = $3,650.00

Remaining 3,200 km × $0.67 = $2,144.00

Total allowable: $5,794.00

The 5,000 km threshold resets every calendar year. It is not cumulative across years and does not carry over.

What Counts as Business Kilometres?

Business kilometres are travel directly related to earning income. For self-employed workers and employees, this includes:

  • Driving to meet clients, customers, or suppliers
  • Travel between work sites or job locations
  • Deliveries related to your business activity
  • Professional development travel required for your work
  • Travel to a business location that is not your regular place of business

Personal driving is never included: grocery runs, personal appointments, taking children to school, and commuting from home to a fixed regular work location. If you work from home and your home qualifies as your primary place of business, you don't have a commuting problem — every client trip starts from your place of business.

What the CRA Requires in a Motor Vehicle Logbook

The CRA's logbook requirements for motor vehicles are similar to the IRS standard: each business trip needs to be recorded separately with:

  • Date of the trip
  • Destination — city, town, or specific address
  • Purpose of the trip (business reason)
  • Kilometres driven for that trip
  • Odometer at start and end of the trip (or at minimum, annual odometer readings)

You also need your total kilometres driven for the year — business and personal — to calculate your business-use percentage. The CRA will compare your claimed business kilometres against your total odometer movement to verify the percentage is plausible.

Simplified Logbook Option: Once you've kept a full logbook for a "base year," the CRA allows you to use a simplified logbook in subsequent years — tracking only business trips for a representative 3-month period, then extrapolating to the full year. The base year logbook must be kept as long as you use the simplified method. If your business-use percentage changes by more than 10 percentage points from the base year, you need to establish a new base year.

Actual Expenses vs. Per-Kilometre Rate

Self-employed Canadians can choose between two approaches:

Per-Kilometre Allowance Method

Multiply your business kilometres by $0.73 (first 5,000) and $0.67 (after). This is simpler but may understate your actual costs, especially if you drive a newer or more expensive vehicle, have high insurance premiums, or did significant repairs this year.

Actual Expense Method

Claim your actual vehicle operating costs (gas, oil, insurance, registration, maintenance, lease payments or Capital Cost Allowance for owned vehicles), multiplied by your business-use percentage. This requires more receipts but typically produces a higher deduction for high-cost vehicles.

For a vehicle costing $0.45–$0.55/km to operate (a common range for Canadian drivers in 2026), the $0.73/km prescribed rate is generous relative to actual cost. Run the numbers for your specific situation — in most cases, the actual expense method wins for higher-cost vehicles and the per-kilometre method wins for lower-cost vehicles.

Provincial Differences to Know

While the CRA sets the federal prescribed rates, some provincial tax systems have their own rules for provincial income tax purposes. Quebec, for instance, uses different capital cost allowance limits and may have different allowable maximums for certain vehicle costs. If you file in Quebec or have significant vehicle expense claims, a tax professional familiar with your province's rules can ensure you're maximizing the claim on both federal and provincial returns.

What Happens If You Don't Have a Logbook

The CRA can and does disallow vehicle expense claims when a logbook isn't available during an audit. Unlike some other countries, Canada does not have an equivalent to the IRS "Cohan rule" — there's no provision for estimating business kilometres without supporting records. If you can't produce a logbook, the deduction is gone.

The CRA's audit selection for small business vehicle expenses is active. Claims that show 90–100% business use, unusually high kilometres relative to the nature of the business, or large deductions relative to reported income are common triggers.
