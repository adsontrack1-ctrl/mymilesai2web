Realtor Mileage Deductions 2026: What Agents Can (and Can't) Deduct

May 2026  ·  8 min read

Real estate agents drive more than almost any other self-employed professional — showings, open houses, listing appointments, closings, broker meetings, continuing education, property inspections. A busy agent easily puts 15,000 to 25,000 business miles on their vehicle in a year. At the 2026 IRS standard mileage rate of $0.725 per mile, that's $10,875 to $18,125 in potential deductions. Most agents claim far less than they're entitled to.

The problem isn't the tax code — it's the records. Here's what qualifies as deductible mileage, what doesn't, and how to build a log that protects your deductions.

The Golden Rule: You Need a Regular Place of Business

Before cataloging deductible trips, you need to understand the most important concept in real estate mileage deductions: your regular place of business determines what counts as commuting (never deductible) versus business travel (deductible).

For most employees, the regular place of business is their employer's office. For real estate agents, it depends on how you practice:

  • If you have a qualifying home office under IRC Section 280A — a space used regularly and exclusively for business — your home is your principal place of business. Every drive from home to a client, property, or meeting is a deductible business trip with no commuting problem.
  • If you work from a broker's office and don't have a qualifying home office, your broker's office is your regular place of business. Driving from home to the broker's office is commuting. Driving from the broker's office to a client property is deductible.
  • If you have multiple offices — a home office and a broker's office — trips between them can be deductible business travel.

The home office rule is the most valuable real estate tax strategy most agents underutilize. If you have a dedicated space — a spare bedroom, a finished basement area, a converted garage — that you use regularly and exclusively for your real estate business (client calls, paperwork, marketing, MLS research), you likely qualify. Consult a tax professional to confirm, but this one election changes the deductibility of every commute you make.

What a Real Estate Mileage Log Should Look Like

IRS Publication 463 requires four elements for each trip: date, miles, destination, and business purpose. For real estate agents, a complete entry might look like:

  • Date: March 14, 2026
  • Miles: 18.4
  • Destination: 4821 Maple Lane, Thornfield
  • Purpose: Buyer showing — Johnson family, 3-bed listing

Notice the specificity: an address, client name, and brief purpose. Compare this to what many agents actually write: "showing" or "client." The latter gives an auditor almost no verification anchor. The former is defensible.

Calculating the Value of Your Deductions

At the 2026 standard mileage rate of $0.725 per mile, here's what common weekly driving patterns add up to annually:

Annual Deduction Estimate — Active Agent

4 showings/week × 12 miles avg roundtrip = 48 mi/week

2 listing appointments/week × 18 miles avg = 36 mi/week

1 open house/week × 14 miles roundtrip = 14 mi/week

Closings, meetings, education = ~20 mi/week

Total: ~118 miles/week × 52 weeks = 6,136 business miles

6,136 mi × $0.725 = $4,449 deduction

A high-volume agent doing 20+ showings per week in a suburban or rural market can easily reach 25,000 business miles per year — an $18,125 deduction. The only thing standing between that deduction and the IRS is a log.

Standard Mileage vs. Actual Expenses: Which Is Better for Realtors?

You have two options for deducting vehicle expenses under the IRS rules:

Standard Mileage Rate Method

Multiply your total business miles by $0.725. Simple, requires minimal record-keeping beyond the mileage log itself. Works especially well if you drive a fuel-efficient or older vehicle (your deduction per mile may exceed your actual cost per mile).

Actual Expense Method

Track and deduct the actual costs of operating your vehicle — gas, insurance, oil changes, tires, registration, and depreciation — multiplied by your business-use percentage. Also requires a mileage log to calculate the business percentage. This method favors newer vehicles with high depreciation and agents who drive expensive vehicles with high operating costs.

Key constraint: If you use the standard mileage rate in the first year you place a vehicle in service, you can switch to actual expenses in a later year. But if you start with actual expenses, you cannot switch back to standard mileage for that vehicle. Most tax advisors recommend starting with standard mileage to preserve flexibility.

Multiple Vehicles and Team Situations

If you operate as a team or use multiple vehicles, the rules still apply per-vehicle. You cannot combine mileage across vehicles to claim on one. Each vehicle tracked for business use needs its own mileage log for the year, including odometer readings at January 1 and December 31 (or placed-in-service and disposition dates if the vehicle changed during the year).

If you drive both a personal vehicle and a company car (broker-owned), you can only deduct expenses for the vehicle you own or lease. You cannot deduct mileage on a vehicle your broker provides.

Keeping Records That Survive an Audit

Real estate agents are at higher audit risk for vehicle deductions because the amounts are large relative to income in slow years and because "100% business use" claims are common and often inaccurate. Protect yourself with three practices:

  1. Log every trip at the time of the trip. A mobile app that automatically captures location and distance when you start driving is the most reliable method. Review and categorize trips daily or weekly — don't let a backlog accumulate.
  2. Never claim 100% business use unless it's genuinely true. If you use the same car for grocery runs and personal trips, your business percentage will be something less than 100%. Claiming 100% when it isn't accurate is one of the fastest ways to attract scrutiny.
  3. Keep supporting records. Client appointment calendars, signed listing agreements, showing confirmations, and closing documents all corroborate your mileage log. Your log is the primary record; everything else makes it bulletproof.

The $0.725/mile deduction is one of the most straightforward tax benefits available to real estate agents. The only thing most agents need to do differently is keep a better log.
