The 2026 IRS Mileage Rate, Explained in Plain English

April 2026 · 6 min read

The IRS sets a standard mileage rate each year for business driving. For 2026, that rate is $0.725 per mile (IRS standard mileage rates). For every business mile you drive, you can deduct 72.5 cents from your taxable income.

If you drove 10,000 business miles in 2026, your deduction is $7,250. That's $7,250 of income you won't pay tax on — at the federal level, and in most states that follow federal AGI.

The 2026 IRS standard mileage rate is $0.725 per mile for business driving. The rate is set annually by the IRS and is designed to cover the average cost of operating a vehicle, including fuel, depreciation, insurance, and maintenance.

Who can use the standard mileage rate?

The standard mileage rate is available to:

  • Self-employed workers filing Schedule C (sole proprietors, freelancers, 1099 contractors)
  • Partners in a partnership who use their personal vehicle for the partnership's business
  • S-corp shareholders who own more than 2% and use a personal vehicle for business

Most W-2 employees cannot use this deduction for 2026. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (Pub. L. 115-97) suspended the unreimbursed employee expense deduction for most workers through at least 2025. If you're an employee whose employer doesn't reimburse vehicle costs, you likely cannot deduct them at the federal level — though some states still allow it. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

Which miles actually qualify as business miles?

The IRS is specific about what counts. The driving must be ordinary and necessary for your business — not personal, and not commuting.

Deductible business miles include:

  • Driving from your office (or qualifying home office) to a client site
  • Driving between client locations or job sites
  • Driving to pick up supplies, equipment, or inventory
  • Driving to the bank to make business deposits
  • Driving to business meetings, conferences, or networking events
  • Driving to a required business-related medical appointment (if self-employed)

Not deductible:

  • Your regular commute from home to your primary place of business
  • Personal errands, even if done on the same day as a business trip
  • Driving your kids to school, grocery runs, or other personal trips

The home office rule changes everything

If your home qualifies as your principal place of business (you regularly and exclusively use a specific area for business and it's your primary place of work), then every trip from your home to a client, vendor, or business location is a deductible business trip — not a commute.

This is one of the most frequently missed deductions. A freelancer who works from home and drives to client meetings can deduct all of those trips. Without a qualifying home office, the first and last trip of the day may be treated as a nondeductible commute.

How to calculate your deduction

The math is simple: multiply your total business miles by $0.725.

2,500 miles$1,812.50
5,000 miles$3,625.00
10,000 miles$7,250.00
15,000 miles$10,875.00
20,000 miles$14,500.00

For most self-employed drivers, the deduction scales linearly with miles tracked: 5,000 documented business miles is $3,625; 10,000 is $7,250. Drivers who don't track systematically typically claim far less than they actually drove, simply because they can't reconstruct the records at filing time.

Standard mileage vs. actual expenses

Standard mileage ($0.725/mile) is one of two methods you can use. The other is the actual expense method: track every dollar you spent on your vehicle — gas, insurance, oil changes, tires, registration fees, and depreciation — then multiply by your business-use percentage.

For most self-employed workers, standard mileage wins or comes close, with far less record-keeping. The exceptions tend to be drivers with very expensive vehicles or very low business use percentages, where actual expenses may be higher than the per-mile rate implies.

Critical limitation: You must elect the standard mileage method in the first year you use a vehicle for business. If you start with actual expenses and take accelerated depreciation (MACRS), you can't switch back to standard mileage for that vehicle later.

What records does the IRS require?

IRS Publication 463 (Travel, Gift, and Car Expenses) governs vehicle deductions. For each business trip, you must document:

  1. Date of the trip
  2. Destination — the business location you drove to
  3. Business purpose — what business was conducted
  4. Miles driven

In addition, record your vehicle's total odometer mileage at the beginning and end of the year. This lets you establish total miles driven, which is used to calculate your business-use percentage if needed.

The IRS places heavy weight on contemporaneous records — logs maintained at or near the time of each trip. Records reconstructed weeks or months after the fact are significantly weaker in an audit. Apps that automatically log trips as they happen produce stronger documentation than anything created retroactively.

What happens if you get audited?

The IRS flags vehicle deductions as one of the most frequently examined items on self-employment returns, particularly when the claimed mileage represents a large portion of income. Auditors will ask to see your mileage log and will compare claimed miles to documented trips.

Round numbers (claiming exactly 10,000 miles, or exactly 80% business use) invite scrutiny. A detailed log with specific dates, destinations, and purposes is far more defensible than estimates.

The bottom line

The standard mileage deduction is one of the largest deductions available to self-employed workers. At $0.725 per mile, 10,000 business miles is a $7,250 reduction in taxable income. The catch is documentation. Vague records, round numbers, and reconstructed logs are audit bait.

The most defensible approach is a contemporaneous log: track every trip as you drive, note the business purpose, and export a compliant report at tax time. That's a 10-second-per-trip habit that can protect thousands of dollars in deductions.
