Uber Drivers: What Your 1099-K Doesn't Tell You About Deductions

April 2026 · 9 min read

Every January, Uber sends you a 1099-K showing your gross earnings. It looks like a large number. Then you pay self-employment tax on it, and the math suddenly doesn't add up.

Here's what your 1099-K doesn't show: the deductions that can cut your taxable income by 30–50%. Most rideshare and delivery drivers — Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart — leave thousands of dollars unclaimed every year, not because the rules are complicated, but because tracking takes effort they don't have time for.

What your 1099-K actually shows

A 1099-K reports gross payment volume processed through the platform. For Uber drivers, this is the full fare Uber collected from passengers — before Uber's service fee (typically 20–27%), before booking fees, and before any expenses you incurred.

If your 1099-K says $45,000, your actual take-home after Uber's cut might be closer to $33,000–$36,000. That's before subtracting gas, vehicle wear, insurance, and other business expenses.

Your 1099-K is your starting point, not your taxable income. The deductions you claim on Schedule C bring your actual net self-employment income — and your tax bill — down significantly.

For 2026, Uber and most gig platforms issue a 1099-K if you received more than $600 in gross payments (down from the previous $20,000 threshold). Some drivers receive 1099-Ks for the first time and are surprised by the amount shown — remember, it's gross, not net.

Mileage: the deduction most drivers dramatically undercount

For 2026, the IRS standard mileage rate is $0.725 per mile for business driving. For a driver who puts 40,000 miles on their car for work in a year, that's a $29,000 deduction.

The problem: Uber's app only tracks miles during active trips — from pickup to dropoff. It doesn't track the miles you drove to reach that first pickup, or the miles driven between dropoffs while waiting for the next ride request. These "dead miles" or "repositioning miles" are still deductible business miles. Studies of rideshare driver patterns have found this gap typically adds 30–40% to total deductible mileage.

Which miles qualify for rideshare and delivery drivers:

  • En-route miles — from the moment you accept a ride request until you pick up the passenger
  • In-trip miles — from pickup to dropoff (what your platform already tracks)
  • Between-trip miles — miles driven while you're actively logged into the app and available for requests, repositioning to a better area
  • Miles to your first pickup location and miles returning home after your last trip may be partially deductible depending on whether your home qualifies as a business location — get professional guidance on this specific point

For delivery drivers (DoorDash, Instacart, Amazon Flex), the same rules apply: miles between pickup at a restaurant and delivery at a customer's address, plus miles driven to the starting pickup location.

Standard mileage vs. actual expenses: which wins for gig drivers?

You have two methods for deducting vehicle costs:

Standard mileage ($0.725/mile): Simple. Multiply your qualifying business miles by $0.725. This rate implicitly covers gas, depreciation, insurance, oil changes, and normal wear — you don't track those separately.

Actual expense method: Track every car-related dollar: gas, insurance, registration, oil changes, tires, car washes, loan interest (if applicable), and depreciation. Multiply total by your business-use percentage (business miles ÷ total miles).

For most rideshare and delivery drivers, standard mileage wins. The per-mile rate is generous, and you avoid the bookkeeping burden of tracking every gas station receipt and maintenance invoice. The exception: if you own an expensive vehicle with very high actual per-mile costs, run the numbers both ways before choosing.

The one rule you can't ignore: You must elect the standard mileage method in the first year you put a vehicle into service for business. If you start with actual expenses and take accelerated (MACRS) depreciation, you can't switch to standard mileage for that vehicle in a later year.

Other deductions rideshare drivers can claim

Beyond mileage, rideshare and delivery drivers have legitimate deductions that many overlook:

  • Cell phone — the business-use percentage of your monthly bill and device cost. If you use your phone 80% for driving work, you can deduct 80% of those costs. Keep records of your total monthly bill.
  • Phone mount and accessories — if used exclusively for the app, these are 100% deductible business equipment
  • Car washes and detailing — deductible at your business-use percentage. Keeping a clean car is a normal business expense for passenger transport.
  • Water, gum, phone chargers for passengers — deductible as business supplies
  • Parking fees and tolls — fully deductible separately from the standard mileage rate (the rate doesn't cover these)
  • Dash cam and other safety equipment — deductible as business equipment
  • Roadside assistance membership (AAA, etc.) — deductible at business-use percentage
  • Platform fees and service charges — any fees Uber deducts from your payouts can be claimed as business expenses, though they're reflected in your net 1099 income

Self-employment tax: the hidden cost of gig income

Unlike W-2 employees who split FICA taxes with their employer, self-employed workers pay the full 15.3% on their net self-employment income (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare). On $30,000 of net income, that's $4,239 in self-employment tax — before any income tax.

Here's why deductions matter so much: every dollar you deduct reduces both your income tax and your self-employment tax liability. A $7,000 mileage deduction might save $2,000 in income tax and another $1,000 in self-employment tax, for a combined $3,000 saving.

You can also deduct half of your self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1, which further reduces your adjusted gross income.

What records you need to keep

The IRS requires contemporaneous records for vehicle deductions. That means logs maintained at or near the time of each trip, not reconstructed from memory in April. For each business trip:

  1. Date of the trip
  2. Starting location and destination
  3. Business purpose (rideshare trip, delivery, etc.)
  4. Miles driven

Also record your car's total odometer mileage at January 1 and December 31 each year to establish your total miles driven.

Your platform's earnings summary is not a sufficient mileage log on its own — it only captures active-trip miles and doesn't document the repositioning miles, en-route miles, or between-trip miles that are also deductible. The net effect: what a typical Uber driver actually pays in taxes

Here's a simplified example. A driver with a 1099-K showing $42,000 in gross earnings:

ItemAmount
Gross 1099-K income$42,000
Less: Uber's service fee (~23%)-$9,660
Less: Mileage deduction (35,000 mi × $0.725)-$25,375
Less: Phone, supplies, tolls, etc.-$1,800
Net self-employment income~$5,165

Without the mileage deduction, that same driver would show $30,540 in net self-employment income and owe roughly $8,000–$9,000 in combined SE tax and federal income tax. With the mileage deduction properly claimed, the liability drops to under $2,000.

The difference is almost entirely the mileage. And the mileage is only capturable if you tracked it.
